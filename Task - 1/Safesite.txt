Construction is one of the most dangerous industries on the planet, claiming over 60,000 lives every year — roughly one death every nine minutes. The vast majority of these incidents are preventable: a missing helmet, a worker in the wrong zone, a hairline crack nobody caught during last week's inspection. The problem isn't negligence — it's that human attention is finite. A safety officer managing hundreds of workers across a sprawling site, juggling twelve camera feeds and a stack of compliance paperwork, simply cannot be everywhere at once. Fatigue sets in, blind spots emerge, and in the gap between what a human can watch and what's actually happening, people get hurt.

SafeSite closes that gap using computer vision. It connects to the cameras already installed on site and processes every feed in real time — flagging a missing helmet within two seconds, triggering an alert the moment a worker enters a restricted zone, and analysing daily inspection photos for structural cracks or equipment wear before a human reviewer would notice them. Everything runs on compact edge hardware mounted directly to existing cameras, with no cloud dependency, so alerts fire in under 100 milliseconds even on sites with no internet. The goal isn't to replace safety officers — it's to give them a tireless second set of eyes that handles the mechanical work of watching, so the people on the ground can focus on the judgment calls that actually require a human.
